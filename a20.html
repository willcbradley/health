Feedback Loops

Fundamentals are permissionless.

Almost anybody can teach themselves almost anything, particularly with the advent of recent technologies.

Why is this important?

Because, for as long as we can deal in fundamentals, we have a timeless fallback plan. Even if more proximal opportunities fall through, we can lean on time-tested knowledge as a foundation for new opportunities.

We've covered all this before. Why discuss it again?

Because, in order to lean into medical opportunities, it helps to have fast feedback loops — close associations between work and reward. The faster (and larger) these rewards, the better.

And, in order to get these feedback loops, we need opportunities. Which means leaning in to the medical game.

This might seem risky. After all, what if the status game falls through?

But as we've talked about before: when we're grounded in fundamentals, our downside is capped. Even a small, slow compounding of foundational principles adds up quickly, and stops us from being dependent on fragile games.

And because we aren't dependent, we can go all in. Because we've thought things through from first principles, we can play the game without fear.

Foundations remove dependence. Independence lets us aim high. Aiming high grants us access to great opportunities. Great opportunities shorten feedback loops.

And short feedback loops are upstream of everything else.
