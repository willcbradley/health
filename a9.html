Earnestness As Exit

Opportunities are fake.

Not "fake" in the sense that they don't exist or aren't valuable. But "fake" in the sense that they're entirely rooted in status games.

If opportunities depended only on the laws of nature, they wouldn't be called opportunities. They'd just be work.

Why does this matter?

In medicine, a lot of career progression depends on opportunities. Because "being a good doctor" is hard to directly measure, we become dependent on having various doors opened for us, often (but not always) based on proxy metrics and/or luck.

And as far as I can tell, depending on status games to make or break a career is a really bad idea.

How to get around this?

By eliminating all dependence. Creating a plan that requires no arbitrary allocation of opportunities. If doors are opened, that's great. But it's no longer a necessity.

To avoid being trapped in useless work, we need to plan for two extremes: never getting any opportunities to do real work, and getting so many that they become a distraction.

The solution to both is the same: prioritising non-pointless work over status games, regardless of context. Reasoning from principles, and taking the work seriously. Being earnest.

It's extremely painful (and often very isolating) to care about things deeply. It makes us prime targets for status ploys, and removes the option of cheap cynicism.

To the degree that I'm earnest, I'm betting that it's worth it.
