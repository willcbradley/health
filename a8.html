Self-Conscious Computers

Nature takes no prisoners.

There's no room for assumptions, no luxury of man-made simplicity. Is the patient alive? Will they ever go home? How certain are you?

Recent centuries have seen unprecedented improvements in computing. But our machines aren't alive.

I don't treat my phone with the same respect I treat my dog. Would you?

Somewhere, amidst all the DNA, and proteins, and fluids, is us.

We are unbelievably complex phenomena, so sophisticated that we can question the meaning of our own existence. How could anything be more interesting than that? Why isn't this the only thing anyone ever talks about?

When we study medicine, we're studying the principles of this impossible system.

The skills we learn have immediate utility, and the concepts we grapple with are beyond timeless.

Have you ever watched a toddler take his first steps? Or seen a bird take off into flight? Or walked through a cool, shady forest with trees the size of small skyscrapers?

As of writing, we have no formal definition of "life". But whatever it is, it's worth protecting. Not through defensive, apologetic stasis, but through competent, optimistic expansion toward an infinite horizon.

Above all, our obligation is to humanity.

By nature of its complexity, medicine is a prime breeding ground for bureaucracy, pseudowork, and rent-seeking. But this is no excuse. Disciplinary boundaries only exist in theory — nobody (except ourselves) can stop us from the chasing the most important problems, no matter where they lead.

There's no shortage of suffering. Everywhere I look, I see people in pain. It's not good enough. These are solvable problems. We can fix them, if we work hard enough.
