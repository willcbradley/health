Intangibles

Now we face a new problem.

Medicine is full of day-to-day demands that pull us away from fundamentals. It requires us to pour effort into perishable projects, sometimes for years at a time.

Is this leading us down a dead end?

No.

In the process of this work, we can learn the principles of fields that are foundational to medicine, like biochemistry, physiology, and pathology.

But as we've discussed previously, these principles are finite. What happens when we run out? Sure, we can branch out into other fields, but what's the point of the repetitive work that remains?

Regulatory protection, as far as I can tell. Medical training gives us access to opportunities that can't be found elsewhere - problems to solve, people to meet, and (most notably) patients to care for.

Once we've worked through the fundamentals, we're essentially trading repetitive drudgery for this protection. For as long as the trade makes sense, no work is wasted.

But what if the trade breaks?

Of course, we can shift our attention elsewhere - but is all our previous work wasted?

Again: no.

There's more to the world than fundamentals. The gap between understanding and doing is filled with intangibles. Heuristics, instincts, and traits that can only be learned through experience.

What's the result of all this?

First, we ground ourself in foundations of medicine. Then, excess effort can be used to explore new fields, and draw new connections. In the meantime, the leftover repetitive work functions as payment for ongoing regulatory protection. If this protection loses value, we can turn our attention elsewhere, but no matter what happens, we develop crucial, intangible skills in the process.

Nobody is forcing us to do anything. Either we go all-in, or we go somewhere else entirely.
