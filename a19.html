Both, Not Either

Fundamentals demand context.

To do anything, we need to apply our ideas in the real world. But in the real world, change is constant.

So, regardless of fundamentals, we're continually required to make incremental, imperfect adjustments based on new information.

Because of this, not all work can be planned out in advance.

Strategy without execution leaves everything to rot, and execution without strategy sends us off in the wrong direction entirely.

I've spent a long time trying to find the optimal balance between these two. And my conclusion is that it doesn't exist.

Because this balance is predicated on context, it's entirely situational. We can't know how much planning is required until we execute, and we can't know how much execution is needed until we plan.

How to manage this impossible balance?

By solving for cycle time.

Collapsing the time between getting new data, reflecting on them, and applying these learnings to generate even newer data.

This is a hard problem. And unsurprisingly, it can't be figured out in advance. But it's also very freeing.

All this time, I've been trying to figure out where to go all in. Whether to throw myself fully into the medical game, or sit back and try and map out the problem space in advance. The answer is both.

Neither are wrong. Both are correct. The bottleneck is constantly shifting back and forth.

The way forward is to attack them simultaneously.
