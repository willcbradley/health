Where Are The Role Models?

I've met some great doctors.

People who are excellent communicators, or skilled proceduralists, or seasoned leaders.

Why, then, aren't their examples inspiring enough to motivate me through my day-to-day studies?

Doctors are doctors because of regulatory protection. Earning this protection involves jumping through hoops — often these hoops are difficult, but they're nonetheless minimum standards.

The incentive is to reach these minimums, but there isn't a huge reason to go further.

Of course, many people go far above and beyond, making huge sacrifices for their patients' sake. But this relies on goodwill, which seems like too fragile a foundation for the future of healthcare.

Why should we pour ourselves into our work, when the incentives to do so are dwindling?

Maybe we need something more solid.

People to emulate, not just because of their skills, but because they can get outsized returns on these skills, above and beyond the bare minimum.

What would such a person look like?

First, they'd be a great doctor. Trusted by patients, respected by peers, and helpful to juniors.

Second, they'd be formidable. Grounded solidly in the fundamentals of medicine, both scientifically and morally. The kind of person who could be dropped into any society, with no money or connections, and figure out a way of producing net value for the world. Somebody who's better at real work than they are at rent-seeking.

And then, almost as a byproduct, they'd get their regulatory protection.

Instead of being an end goal, it'd become a requisite disciplinary process that lays the foundation for work to come.




I've spent years looking for a doctor that inspires me enough to focus all my bandwidth on medicine. I can't find one. So instead, I'll try to become him. Time will tell if I succeed (and to the degree that I fail, maybe others will pick up where I left off).

Without true role models, medicine will go to zero. And if this happens, I suspect we'll probably go extinct.
