Drudgery

Historically, boring work was safe.

If something was worth doing one day, it was also worth doing the next.

But the pace of technological change has broken this assumption. We can no longer afford to assume that things will stay static.

So, in response to this, it's tempting to avoid doing anything repetitive.

After all: why bother with dreary work, if it'll be automated tomorrow?

The answer is nuanced. The least valuable work is often repetitive and dreary, but so are the non-pointless fundamentals that compound over time.

The challenge lies in figuring out which is which, and then having enough conviction to invest time accordingly.

It's essentially a maturity problem, as far as I can tell. Like a baby learning object permanence, we need to learn that time invested in the right places yield return, despite how risky it feels to pour time down the drain as the world speeds up.

And of course, in the meantime, time is passing anyway.

What does this all mean?

Blindly trusting "safe" work creates unacceptable blowup risk.

Arrogantly avoiding drudgery leaves us with an unacceptably small chance of success.

The only way forward is to chase the right kind of drudgery. And the cost of failure has never been higher.
